Battle of Gettysburg — Faculty Facilitator Guide

U.S. Army War College Staff Ride · Decision Points, Discussion Questions & Teaching Notes · Companion to the Staff Ride Simulator

The March North 3–30 June 1863

Theme: Strategy & Operational Maneuver — ends, ways, means; the cavalry as the commander's eyes.

Learning objectives
  • Trace how Lee's strategic aim shaped the operational maneuver into Pennsylvania.
  • Assess the consequence of Stuart's absence on Lee's situational understanding.
  • Evaluate command turbulence: Hooker→Meade three days before contact.

Staff-ride linkage: Stand: Eisenhower Farm overlook / the approach roads — orient on the road network that funneled both armies to this crossroads.

⏸ Discussion — Before the Campaign — Frame the Problem

Before the action:

  1. What are Lee's strategic ENDS, and do his operational WAYS (a raid into Pennsylvania) match his MEANS?
  2. What strategic assumptions is Lee making about Northern morale and the political clock?
  3. How does the geography — the Blue Ridge / South Mountain screen and the road net — enable the maneuver?

⚔ Decision Point — The Cavalry's Orders (Gen. Lee, Confederate)

Stuart proposes riding around the Union army to screen your right and gather supplies, rather than guarding the passes and reporting enemy movements. Your infantry is strung out across the mountains in enemy country.

  • Tie the cavalry tight to the army — You preserve your reconnaissance — but surrender the audacity that has defined your campaigns, and the discretion you've always granted trusted subordinates.
  • Grant Stuart his discretionary ride (historical) — Stuart loses contact for a week. You advance blind and stumble into a meeting engagement on ground you did not choose.
  • Order a wide raid but keep two brigades — A reasonable hedge — yet the brigades Lee actually retained (Jones, Robertson) were his least aggressive, and they failed to keep him informed.

What happened: Lee's orders were permissive and ambiguous; Stuart chose the bold ride and was gone until the evening of Day 2. Lee fought the most important battle of the war without his cavalry.

Teaching point: INTELLIGENCE & MISSION COMMAND. Discretionary orders demand a shared understanding of the commander's intent. When the priority (reconnaissance) is not made explicit and non-negotiable, a trusted subordinate may optimize for the wrong thing. The cavalry is the commander's eyes — blinding yourself forfeits the initiative.

⏸ Discussion — Command Turbulence on the Eve of Battle

Before the action:

  1. What are the risks and opportunities of changing army commanders 72 hours before a decisive battle?
  2. Meade must satisfy two masters — protect Washington AND defeat Lee. How do competing strategic guidance and a new commander's caution interact?
  3. Both armies are now converging on a road hub neither chose. How do road networks and logistics drive operational outcomes?

Day 1 — Meeting Engagement 1 July 1863

Theme: Meeting Engagement & Mission Command — initiative, intent, and discretionary orders.

Learning objectives
  • Explain how a meeting engagement develops from a movement to contact.
  • Analyze Buford's delay as economy of force buying time and terrain.
  • Judge Ewell's 'if practicable' order as a problem of mission command.

Staff-ride linkage: Stand 1–3: McPherson Ridge, Oak Ridge, Barlow's Knoll, then Cemetery Hill.

⚔ Decision Point — Defend the Ridges, or Fall Back? (Brig. Gen. Buford, Union)

You have ~2,900 troopers against a Confederate infantry division of 7,000+ with more behind it. South of town stand Cemetery Hill, Culp's Hill, and the ridges — ground worth an army. Reynolds' infantry is hours away.

  • Make a dismounted stand on the ridges (historical) — Your delay buys the hours Reynolds needs. The army inherits the fishhook — the decision that may win the battle.
  • Screen and withdraw south — You keep your division intact but surrender the heights uncontested — Confederates may hold Cemetery Hill before the Union infantry arrives.
  • Mount and counterattack — Cavalry charging deployed infantry is a recipe for slaughter; you squander your one asset — the ability to delay from cover.

What happened: Buford fought dismounted from the ridges, his carbines and the terrain delaying Heth until Reynolds' I Corps arrived mid-morning. It is the model defensive delay.

Teaching point: ECONOMY OF FORCE & TERRAIN. A delay is not a retreat — it is buying time and protecting decisive terrain with minimum force. Buford's reading of the ground (KOCOA: key terrain to the south) shaped the entire battle before the main bodies arrived.

⚔ Decision Point — Cemetery Hill — 'If Practicable' (Lt. Gen. Ewell, Confederate)

It is early evening. The Union corps are routed and streaming onto Cemetery Hill, but they are rallying on strong, rising ground. Lee's order: take the hill 'if practicable,' but avoid bringing on a general engagement. Your divisions are disorganized by victory; Union reinforcements are arriving; the ground is steep.

  • Press the attack at once — A bold stroke that MIGHT seize the keystone of the fishhook — or break your disordered, tired divisions against fresh troops on high ground. History never ran this test.
  • Judge it impracticable; consolidate (historical) — The Union keeps Cemetery and Culp's Hill — the anchors of the position they will hold for two more days. Critics call it the lost opportunity of the battle.
  • Take Culp's Hill instead — Culp's Hill dominates Cemetery Hill and the Baltimore Pike; unoccupied at dusk, it was arguably the real prize — but Johnson's division arrived late and the chance closed.

What happened: Ewell judged an assault impracticable and did not press. Whether the hill could have been taken is the battle's most enduring 'what-if.' Jackson, his predecessor, might have attacked — the comparison haunted Ewell.

Teaching point: MISSION COMMAND & DISCRETIONARY ORDERS. 'If practicable' delegates judgment but transfers risk to a subordinate who may lack the senior's intent and audacity. Effective mission command requires a clearly communicated intent and a subordinate empowered — and willing — to seize fleeting opportunity. The order's ambiguity, not Ewell alone, is on trial.

⚔ Decision Point — Gettysburg or the Pipe Creek Line? (Maj. Gen. Meade, Union)

You took command three days ago. Two corps are bloodied but the army holds superb defensive ground. Your engineers have prepared a strong fallback — the Pipe Creek Line — closer to your supply base and covering Washington and Baltimore. The rest of your corps are marching through the night.

  • Stand and fight at Gettysburg (historical) — You fight on interior lines and commanding ground of your choosing. The compact fishhook lets you shift reserves faster than Lee can shift around its exterior.
  • Withdraw to Pipe Creek — Tactically sound on paper — but you surrender the moral initiative and the finest defensive ground on the field, and risk being caught mid-withdrawal.

What happened: Meade ordered the army to concentrate at Gettysburg and ratified the decision in a council of war the next night — choosing to fight on the fishhook. Interior lines proved decisive.

Teaching point: INTERIOR LINES & DECISION UNDER UNCERTAINTY. The fishhook gave Meade shorter reinforcement distances than Lee's exterior line. A new commander chose to fight on ground his subordinates had seized — trusting the situation over his own prepared plan. Good positions are sometimes inherited; the art is recognizing and keeping them.

⏸ Discussion — End of Day 1 — Assess

After the action:

  1. Who 'won' Day 1 — and by what measure (ground, casualties, position, initiative)?
  2. Was Ewell's decision a failure of the man, or of the order? How would you have written Lee's order?
  3. How did three separate subordinate decisions (Buford, Reynolds, Howard's reserve, Hancock) shape the field more than either army commander?

Day 2 — The Flanks 2 July 1863

Theme: The Offensive-Defensive & Initiative — synchronization, the human dimension, seizing key terrain.

Learning objectives
  • Compare Lee's offensive-defensive plan with Longstreet's maneuver alternative.
  • Assess Sickles' advance to the Peach Orchard against commander's intent.
  • Derive principles of leadership and initiative from Little Round Top.

Staff-ride linkage: Stand 4–8: Warfield/Seminary Ridge, Peach Orchard, Wheatfield, Devil's Den, Little Round Top.

⚔ Decision Point — Attack the Fishhook — or Maneuver Around It? (Gen. Lee, Confederate)

Meade holds commanding ground on interior lines. Your army is on the exterior. Stuart is still absent; your knowledge of the Union dispositions is thin. Longstreet urges a wide move around the Union left to interpose between Meade and Washington, forcing Meade to attack YOU.

  • Echelon attack on the Union left (historical) — You retain the initiative and the morale of the offensive — but attack strong ground, blind, against an enemy who can reinforce on interior lines faster than you can exploit.
  • Adopt Longstreet's maneuver — Plays to your army's defensive strength and Meade's mandate to cover Washington — but cedes the initiative, risks a march across the enemy's front without cavalry, and your supply situation will not wait.
  • Stand on the defensive in place — Meade, content to hold the better ground, has no reason to attack — and your army cannot feed itself in place indefinitely in enemy country.

What happened: Lee chose the offensive: 'The enemy is there, and I am going to attack him there.' Longstreet executed the assault he had argued against, and the resulting delays and frontal nature of the attack dogged it all day.

Teaching point: THE OFFENSIVE-DEFENSIVE & THE INITIATIVE. Lee valued moral initiative and his army's offensive spirit; Longstreet valued the tactical defensive's lethality (Fredericksburg in reverse). Both read the same map differently. The friction between a commander's intent and a doubting executor degraded synchronization all day.

⚔ Decision Point — Hold the Ridge, or Take the High Ground Forward? (Maj. Gen. Sickles, Union)

Your assigned position on lower southern Cemetery Ridge is dominated by higher ground to your front at the Peach Orchard. You have no explicit orders refining your line, and you fear being shelled from the high ground ahead.

  • Hold the assigned line on Cemetery Ridge — You preserve the integrity and interior lines of the fishhook — the army fights as one connected position.
  • Advance to the Peach Orchard (historical) — You create a vulnerable salient with both flanks in the air, leave Little Round Top open, and invite the destruction of your corps — though your sacrifice absorbs and disorders Longstreet's attack.

What happened: Sickles advanced without authorization. His corps was wrecked (and he lost a leg), but the unexpected salient broke up Longstreet's echelon timing and bought time for reserves to save the line behind him.

Teaching point: COMMANDER'S INTENT & DISCIPLINE. A subordinate who substitutes his own scheme for the higher plan endangers the whole — even when local terrain logic seems sound. Integrity of the line (mutual support, interior lines) outweighs local advantage. Yet note the paradox: the blunder partially absorbed the blow. War rewards no clean morals.

⚔ Decision Point — Out of Ammunition on the Flank (Col. Chamberlain, Union)

You hold the extreme left of the Union army on Little Round Top. The 15th Alabama has charged five times. Your ammunition is gone, a third of your men are down, and another assault is forming to turn your flank — beyond which lies the army's undefended rear.

  • Order the bayonet charge (historical) — The shock of a downhill bayonet charge breaks the exhausted Alabamians and captures hundreds — the flank holds. Audacity at the decisive point.
  • Withdraw to re-supply — Yielding the crest uncovers the army's flank; the Confederates take the hill that enfilades the entire Union line.
  • Hold and fight with what you have — With empty rifles against a fresh assault on your open flank, a static defense likely collapses.

What happened: Chamberlain ordered 'Bayonet!' and a wheeling downhill charge that shattered the assault and saved the flank — a Medal of Honor action and the textbook case of small-unit initiative.

Teaching point: INITIATIVE AT THE DECISIVE POINT & THE HUMAN DIMENSION. Three leaders — Warren (foresight), Vincent (rapid commitment), Chamberlain (audacity) — each acted on their own initiative inside the senior commander's intent. Decisive results often turn on junior leaders empowered to act faster than orders can reach them.

⏸ Discussion — After Little Round Top — Leadership

After the action:

  1. Warren, Vincent, and Chamberlain all acted without waiting for orders. What conditions of command climate make that initiative possible?
  2. Contrast Sickles' unauthorized advance with Warren's unauthorized diversion of troops. Why do we praise one and damn the other?
  3. How does the human dimension — fatigue, fear, will — show up across this day more than the maps suggest?

Day 3 — The Charge 3 July 1863

Theme: Culmination & the Decisive Point — concentration, the cost of the sunk-cost trap.

Learning objectives
  • Weigh Lee's decision to assault the center against Longstreet's flank proposal.
  • Explain culmination and the limits of the offensive.
  • Connect the repulse to the campaign's strategic outcome.

Staff-ride linkage: Stand 9–11: Confederate line on Seminary Ridge, the field of Pickett's Charge, The Angle / High-Water Mark.

⚔ Decision Point — Renew the Assault — on the Center? (Gen. Lee, Confederate)

Day 2 failed on both flanks. Your army is bloodied; ammunition and supplies are dwindling in enemy country; Stuart has finally arrived. Longstreet pleads, again, to disengage and maneuver around the Union left rather than assault the center across open ground. You believe Meade has stripped his center to hold the flanks.

  • Assault the center — Pickett's Charge (historical) — A frontal assault across ¾ mile of open ground into massed artillery and infantry on high ground. It fails catastrophically — over 50% losses — and ends the offensive.
  • Maneuver around the Union left (Longstreet) — Preserves the army and plays to its strength — but cedes the initiative, is logistically fragile in enemy country, and Meade may simply hold his superb ground.
  • Withdraw toward Virginia now — Concedes the campaign and the moral defeat — but preserves the Army of Northern Virginia as a force-in-being. The army would fight on for two more years.

What happened: Lee, trusting his men's offensive spirit and convinced the center was the weak point, ordered the charge. It failed. 'It is all my fault,' he told the survivors.

Teaching point: CULMINATION & THE SUNK-COST TRAP. An army that has reached its culminating point cannot generate decisive offensive results; pressing on converts a stalled offensive into a catastrophe. The hardest command decision is to STOP — to recognize that prior investment (two days of attacks) does not justify the next, costlier one.

⏸ Discussion — Before the Charge — Culmination

Before the action:

  1. Has Lee's army reached its culminating point? What indicators would tell a commander he has?
  2. Lee is influenced by two days of near-misses and faith in his men. How does the sunk-cost trap operate on commanders?
  3. Hunt husbanded his guns and ceased fire to deceive. How does disciplined economy of a scarce resource decide battles?

Culmination & Aftermath 4 July – consequences

Theme: Strategic Assessment — was it decisive? Tactical victory vs. strategic effect; the pursuit problem.

Learning objectives
  • Assess Gettysburg coupled with Vicksburg as a strategic turning point.
  • Critique Meade's pursuit and the limits of decisive victory.
  • Translate the campaign's lessons to the contemporary operating environment.

Staff-ride linkage: Stand: Soldiers' National Cemetery — the Gettysburg Address and the war's redefined purpose.

⚔ Decision Point — Press the Pursuit, or Secure the Victory? (Maj. Gen. Meade, Union)

Lee is retreating, his back to the flooded Potomac at Williamsport. Your own army is exhausted and bled white — you have lost 23,000 men and many senior commanders. Lincoln wants Lee's army DESTROYED, not merely defeated.

  • Attack the trapped army aggressively — The decisive blow Lincoln craves — but against an entrenched, desperate enemy with your own army wrecked and disorganized, a repulse could squander the victory.
  • Pursue cautiously (historical) — You preserve your battered army and confirm the victory — but Lee escapes across the Potomac to fight for two more years. Lincoln: 'We had them within our grasp.'

What happened: Meade pursued deliberately; Lee escaped across the Potomac on 13–14 July. Meade was widely criticized for not destroying the army, though his own force was severely damaged.

Teaching point: DECISIVE VICTORY & THE PURSUIT. The destruction of the enemy army — not just the seizure of a field — is what makes victory strategically decisive. Yet a victorious army is often too damaged to pursue. The gap between TACTICAL success and STRATEGIC effect is the campaign's final, enduring lesson.

⏸ Discussion — Strategic Assessment — Was Gettysburg Decisive?

After the action:

  1. Coupled with Vicksburg, was Gettysburg the 'turning point' — or does that label flatten a war with two more years to run?
  2. Distinguish Lee's TACTICAL defeat from the STRATEGIC effect of the campaign. What did the North actually gain?
  3. Map the campaign's lessons — intelligence, mission command, culmination, the offensive-defensive, civil-military friction — onto a contemporary operating problem your seminar is studying.
  4. If you commanded either army, what is the single decision you would change, and what second-order effects would it create?
